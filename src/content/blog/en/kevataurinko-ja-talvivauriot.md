Today the weather was gorgeous — bright sunshine, the snow started to melt a little, and the crust was firm enough to walk on. The thermometer read +18 °C in the sun, with a maximum of 19.2 degrees.

![Thermometer showing +18 °C](../../../images/20260307_142602.jpg)

## The danger of spring sun

Although a warm spring day feels wonderful, it can be dangerous for fruit trees. When the sun heats the trunk strongly during the day and the temperature drops sharply below freezing at night, the bark can crack. This is known as frost cracking. In the worst case, the damage can kill the tree.

The damage typically appears right at the snow line — just above the snow cover, where the sun's warming effect is strongest. We often wonder which slope is better for fruit trees — south-facing or north-facing. Our farm has both, and each has its advantages.

## Protection

We wrap the trunks with white fine-mesh plastic netting. It protects against three threats at once: sun damage, vole damage, and hare damage.

## Assessing winter damage

The harsh winter has left its mark. When we prune branches, we can immediately see how the tree survived the winter — a healthy branch has green cambium, a damaged one has brown.

![Branch cross-section showing cambium layer](../../../images/20260307_105002.jpg)

European pear varieties fared particularly poorly. Even the Minnesota-bred Summercrisp pear suffered significant damage. In contrast, Russian pear varieties — Chizhovskaya, Lada, Tatyana, and Velesa — are completely unharmed and green on the cut surface.

Among apple varieties, Gala, Rubín, and Topaz suffered damage. Czech varieties are not reliable in this climate.
